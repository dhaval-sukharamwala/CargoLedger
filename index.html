01 · Situation

Getting paid was a game of telephone.

One invoice, one slow lap between two teams — and one wrong field restarted it. I mapped the loop before designing anything: the delay was a handoff problem, not a screen problem.

Rejected

One wrong field killed the file

A missing container number or billing address bounced the whole invoice back. Massive delays and a mountain of billing errors, all from a single typo upstream.

Two teams, two languages

Operations thought in cargo — DOs, containers, addresses. Accounts thought in money — base amounts, GST, surcharges. The spreadsheet forced each to wade through the other's data to find its own.

Why the business cared · 01

Cash trapped in the loop

Every lap of the relay was a day the finished job stayed unbilled. Revenue that was already earned sat as email attachments instead of receivables.

Why the business cared · 02

Hours burned on re-keying

Accounts re-typed what Operations had already typed. Two teams paid twice for the same data entry, and the second copy is where the errors crept in.

Why the business cared · 03

Client trust on the line

A wrong invoice reaching a client becomes a dispute — and a client double-checking every future invoice. Errors weren't just slow; they were reputational.

Key takeaway

The delay was never a screen problem. It was two teams forced to share one document that served neither of them.

02 · Research

Two teams who never needed the same information.

I mapped what each team used — and what each ignored. The two ignore-lists barely overlapped, and that gap became the design.

Upstream · Operations

Operations

Owns the cargo facts
  • Delivery orders, container codes, billing parties, drop-off addresses.
  • Keys those details in the moment a job is done.
  • Every typo here becomes a rejected invoice downstream.
"I just need to log the cargo once and move on."
Downstream · Accounts

Accounts

Turns cargo into money
  • Base amounts, GST, surcharges, totals.
  • Re-keys everything from the emailed sheet, then drafts.
  • Rejects the whole file if a single figure or reference is off.
"If the maths isn't transparent, I can't sign it off."

03 · The brief I set

One screen both teams could trust.

Replace the relay with a single module. Three rules, and every later decision judged against them.

Rule 01 · the one everything else serves

One shared page

Both teams work from the exact same live record, at the same time. No files emailed back and forth, no version drift, no "which sheet is current?" Every other decision on this page is this rule, applied.

Rule 02

No information overload

Each team sees only what its job needs — cargo for Operations, money for Accounts — and nothing it has to scroll past.

Rule 03

Crystal-clear maths

Cost breakdowns detailed enough to trust at a glance, so mistakes get caught before they reach the customer, not after.

What changed

The spreadsheet and the email chain are gone. Cargo pre-fills from the job record, tax calculates itself, and both teams read the same live invoice.

Why it changed

Every error I traced came from a copy — re-typing, re-attaching, re-sending. Remove the copies and the errors have nowhere to enter.

What improved

Rejections stopped restarting the flow, drafts appear the moment cargo is logged, and disputes reaching clients fell 46%.

07 · Explored, then rejected

Three ways to kill the relay. Two of them failed on paper.

"One record, two reads" wasn't the first idea — it was the survivor of three, eliminated by the rules.

Option A

One table for everyone

Every cargo column and every money column in a single master grid. Honest, complete — and enormous.

Rejected

It breaks Rule 02 on day one: each team scrolls past the other's half to find its own. That overload was the original complaint, rebuilt in software.

Option B

Two separate modules

A dedicated ops tool and a dedicated accounts tool, each perfectly tailored, passing the invoice between them.

Rejected

It breaks Rule 01: two tools means two records, and a handoff between them. That's the email relay again, just with nicer screens — version drift included.

Final direction

One record, two reads

A single shared record underneath; each team works in a view shaped for its job — cargo for Operations, money for Accounts.

Chosen

The only structure that passes all three rules: one source of truth, no overload, and the maths lives on the same record both teams sign off.

08 · The core move

The same record, read two ways.

Each team works in the screen its job needs — Operations logs cargo, Accounts reads money — but both run off one shared record, so the two can't drift.

09 · The shared overview

One live picture, not a spreadsheet hunt.

Both teams land here first: what’s outstanding, what just moved, where the revenue sits — one click from any row into the full invoice.

The live overview · the screen both teams open first

One change I’d make in a v2: the four headline numbers are net revenue, MRR, active customers and outstanding. Two of them, MRR and active-customers, are subscription metrics, and this is a receivables desk, not a subscription business. I’d swap them for what Accounts actually chases: overdue aging and average days-to-pay. It’s the first thing I’d redraw.

01

Cargo, not data entry

Operations confirms what the system already knows — it never re-types it — so the typo that used to sink the file has nowhere to enter.

The trade-offPre-filling moves the point of failure upstream: if the source data is wrong, confirming it launders the error instead of catching it. That is exactly why the preview step exists — a human still sees the finished invoice before anything commits.

02

Auto-drafted invoices

The moment cargo is confirmed, the invoice generates, mapping each line automatically instead of waiting on Accounts to key it in by hand.

The trade-offAuto-drafting can feel like the system took over. So nothing commits until preview and submit — an extra step I kept on purpose, because with money, one deliberate confirmation beats a fast mistake.

03

Grouped the way clients expect

Split by DO, Consolidated or Individual turns an error-prone judgement call into a labelled choice with a clear recommendation.

The trade-offI could have auto-picked the format from the data. I didn't — billing shape is a call clients care about, and hiding it in logic would trade a visible, correctable choice for an invisible one. So it's an explicit option with a sensible default, not a guess buried in code.

04

Nothing final until it's chosen

"Modify selection" steps back without losing context; nothing commits until submit. A money flow should be forgiving — both teams can explore formats freely, because rejection no longer means restarting.

One decision, at full depth

Why you cannot skip the preview. The fastest version of this flow commits the invoice the moment Operations confirms the cargo — no preview, one click, done. I rejected it, and it’s the decision I’d defend longest in any interview.

The case for skipping was real: an extra step on every invoice, including the clean majority that would sail through. The case against came from the data path. Pre-filling means the system repeats whatever the job record says — and with no preview, a wrong upstream value gets laundered into a client-facing document at machine speed. The old relay was slow, but its email-back-to-check step was a human review; killing the relay meant keeping that review while losing everything around it.

So preview became the one deliberate step in an otherwise automatic flow. The seeded-error test in the validation section is where it earned its keep — and where it failed: reviewers still missed a wrong tariff, so the answer wasn’t removing the step, it was arming it with the deviation flag. The −46% lives or dies on this one screen.

Itemised & auditable

Every container, HSN code, tariff and tax line sits beside a bill summary and grand total — with "created by & on" always visible, so there's a clean trail behind every figure.

The trade-offFull itemisation makes the invoice long — a compact summary would fit on one screen. I chose density on purpose: for money, a figure you can trace beats a figure that fits, and the expandable rows keep the length from overwhelming.

Findable at scale

Search, filters and sorting keep thousands of invoices navigable; pagination keeps the table calm rather than endless.

12 · Constraints

The walls the design had to work inside.

These were the fixed walls — and where each one shaped a decision you've already seen above.

C·01

GST compliance & audit trail

Every line needs its HSN code, CGST/SGST split and a created-by trail. This is why the invoice is dense rather than tidy — a figure you can trace beats a figure that fits.

C·02

Zoho Books is the system of record

The invoice isn't done until it syncs to the books, over an integration the product doesn't control. Hence honest per-row sync states with retry.

C·03

Source data quality

Pre-filling is only as good as the job record beneath it. Bad upstream data would be laundered, not caught — which is why preview-before-commit cannot be skipped.

C·04

Ten weeks, one designer

Scope pinned to the create → preview → sync spine: one currency, fulfilled jobs. Multi-currency, credit notes and partial payments were deferred, not forgotten.

14 · Validation & what didn’t work
✓ Task walkthroughs · both teams ✓ Clickable prototype ✓ Stakeholder review · Accounts lead ✗ One test failed → design changed ✓ Post-launch tracking

How I knew it worked before the metrics said so.

Before and after launch, I tested the two claims the design rests on: errors get caught at preview, and each team can work without wading through the other's data.

V·01

Task walkthroughs with both teams

Each team re-raised a real past invoice on the prototype. I watched for hesitation, not opinions. Ops finished without once asking about tax; Accounts never asked what a figure meant.

V·02

The test that failed — and changed the design

I planted a wrong tariff in a preview. Early rounds missed it — which is what produced the inline tariff-deviation flag in the Accounts section. The design changed because the test failed.

V·03

Sign-off from the people who sign off

The Accounts lead walked the cost breakdown line by line before build, against their own words from research: "if the maths isn't transparent, I can't sign it off."

V·04

Tracked after launch

Dispute rate and rejected-invoice counts, before versus after — the numbers in the Result section below. The −46% is the test the live product had to pass.

Right the first time, and paid faster.

16 · What I'd do next

What I'd improve in v2 — and what I'd watch.

What I'd measure to be sure it worked, what I kept out of v1, and where I'd want more proof before trusting it at scale.

The number I'd track to prove it

The headline is fewer disputes, but I'd watch rejected-invoice rate and days-to-pay. If preview is really stopping errors — not moving them downstream — that's where it shows.

What I scoped out of v1

V1 is the create → preview → sync loop for one currency and fulfilled jobs. Partial payments, credit notes and multi-currency each add a branch to the money maths — I'd rather ship the spine solid than the tree shaky.

What I'd validate next

The three invoice formats covered every case I saw, but enterprise billing has a long tail. I'd test against a wider client set before assuming three is enough.

What I’d improve today

Bulk invoice editing for month-end runs · keyboard-first entry for high-volume days · richer audit history per invoice · anomaly flags on tariffs, extending the deviation tooltip · overdue-aging on the dashboard in place of the subscription KPIs.

The open risk

The Zoho sync is where the flow leaves the product. I built the retry and error states, but I'd want real error telemetry at volume before calling that path trustworthy.

The lesson I keep

Looking back, this module was never really about generating invoices — it was about removing ambiguity between two teams who each held half the truth. That’s the part that transfers: most enterprise friction is two roles forced to share an artefact that serves neither. If I carried this work forward, the thread I’d pull is trust at speed — anomaly flags that widen the deviation tooltip, audit history a human can actually read, and billing alerts that reach the team before they reach a client.
